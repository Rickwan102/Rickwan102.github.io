Program learning outcomes

What the program teaches

The MS in Applied Data Science is built around six core learning outcomes that span the full data science lifecycle.

PLO 1
Data collection & storage
Identify and leverage applicable technologies to collect, store, and access data at scale. In my own words: knowing how to find data, store it efficiently, and retrieve it when needed — whether that's a database, a vector store, or raw files.
PLO 2
Actionable insight
Create actionable insight across societal, business, and political contexts using the full data science life cycle. In my own words: the whole point of data science — turning numbers into decisions that actually help people.
PLO 3
Visualization & prediction
Apply visualization techniques and predictive models to help generate and communicate insight. In my own words: using charts, models, and visuals to make patterns visible and predictions useful.
PLO 4
Programming
Use programming languages such as R and Python to support the generation of actionable insight. In my own words: writing code that actually works and solves real problems — not just toy examples.
PLO 5
Communication
Communicate insights via visualization and analytics to a broad range of audiences including sponsors and technical leads. In my own words: being able to explain what you found to both a data scientist and someone who has never written code.
PLO 6
Ethics & fairness
Apply ethics in the development, use, and evaluation of data and predictive models — including fairness, bias, transparency, and privacy. In my own words: making sure the systems we build don't harm people, leak private data, or make unfair decisions.

IST 707 — Applied Machine Learning
Mental Health & Wellbeing Analysis: Predicting Anxiety and Depression Among Syracuse Students
Analyzed responses from 4,957 Syracuse University students in the 2025 Campus Wellness Survey to predict anxiety and depression using machine learning. The goal was to help the Barnes Center shift from reactive crisis management to proactive, data-informed outreach — identifying at-risk students before they reach a crisis point. We built Random Forest classifiers achieving 72.55% accuracy for anxiety and 79.04% for depression, using validated clinical screening tools (GAD-2 for anxiety, PHQ-2 for depression) as target variables while excluding direct symptom measures to prevent data leakage and ensure the models could work proactively on students who hadn't yet disclosed problems.
My specific contribution

I led the clustering analysis portion of the project. I implemented and tuned both DBSCAN and HDBSCAN algorithms, ran a grid search across hyperparameters to optimize silhouette scores, and generated t-SNE visualizations to make the high-dimensional clusters interpretable. I then analyzed and wrote up the cluster profiles — identifying distinct student segments such as the all-male low-anxiety cluster, the lonely high-depression group falling through the cracks of campus services, and the therapy-engaged students with strong social support. These cluster insights formed the basis of our recommendations to the Barnes Center.

Stakeholder
Syracuse University Barnes Center for Health & Wellbeing
Key finding
Loneliness was the #1 predictor of both anxiety and depression — lonely students showed 2–5× higher rates than socially connected peers
Technologies
Python scikit-learn Random Forest DBSCAN HDBSCAN t-SNE

IST 691 — Deep Learning in Practice
Music Recommendations for Indie Artists Using Deep Learning
Built a Convolutional Autoencoder that analyzes short 5-second audio clips to identify acoustically similar songs — bridging mainstream and independent music catalogs. The core problem: listeners on TikTok and Instagram encounter 10–20 second clips but can't find the full track or discover similar indie artists. Existing tools like Shazam only match exact audio, and Spotify's recommendations favor popular artists. Our solution was a deep learning model that learns compact audio embeddings from short snippets, enabling similarity matching without relying on user behavior or popularity data.
My specific contribution

I was responsible for the audio data pipeline. I handled the preprocessing of 8,000 Free Music Archive tracks — splitting each song into 5-second chunks with 50% overlap to generate 83,290 training snippets, converting every chunk into a mel-spectrogram (a 128×216 array representing frequency and loudness over time using Short-Time Fourier Transform), and normalizing all spectrogram values between 0 and 1 so that loud and quiet songs would be comparable. I also implemented the song-level train/validation split to prevent data leakage, ensuring no chunks from the same song appeared in both sets.

Results
70% Top-10 accuracy; when the exact song wasn't retrieved, results were typically from the same genre — showing the embeddings captured meaningful musical similarity
Model
Convolutional Autoencoder with 256-dimensional bottleneck embeddings; validation loss of 0.0042 MSE
Technologies
Python PyTorch Convolutional Autoencoder Mel-spectrograms STFT Free Music Archive

IST 688 — Building Human-Centered AI Applications
AI Tax Assistant: RAG-Powered Chatbot for Federal Tax Guidance
Developed a full-stack AI chatbot that answers tax questions using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) over a custom IRS document vector database, with a specific focus on simplifying tax filing for students — especially international students. Built on Google Gemini 2.5 Flash and LangChain, the system uses a multi-agent architecture: an Orchestrator Agent directing the workflow, an Intake Agent building personalized tax profiles, a RAG Agent retrieving from ChromaDB, a Checklist Agent generating interactive task lists, and a Tool Agent running tax calculations. User data is treated as ephemeral — never logged to a database or used for training — and all uploaded documents are processed with PII detection and masking.
My specific contribution

I contributed approximately 25% of the total project effort across two areas. First, I implemented the chunking strategy for the RAG pipeline — dividing IRS documents into 900-token chunks with 250-token overlap, a configuration I arrived at through testing to preserve context across chunk boundaries without sacrificing retrieval precision. Second, I designed and built the Tool Agent, which provides a tax calculator computing federal tax liability based on income and filing status using 2024 tax brackets and standard deductions. I also initially developed a web search tool but removed it after team discussion to keep the system focused on our curated IRS documentation. Both components were merged into the main orchestrator codebase.

Actionable insight
Personalized, real-time tax guidance grounded in official IRS documents — accessible to students without tax expertise or access to a CPA
Ethics & privacy
Ephemeral user data; automatic PII detection and masking; legal disclaimer on every response; sessions user-controlled and deletable
Technologies
Python Streamlit LangChain Google Gemini RAG ChromaDB EasyOCR HuggingFace

Program track

My academic track

How I shaped my degree around the intersection of AI and human-centered design.

Applied Human-Centered AI + Applied Data Science

From data science to human-centered AI

I began the program on the Applied Data Science track, which gave me a strong technical foundation — learning how to collect, process, and model data using Python, machine learning, and statistical methods. Courses like IST 707 (Applied Machine Learning) grounded me in the fundamentals of building predictive models and interpreting their outputs for real stakeholders.

As the program progressed, I shifted to the Applied Human-Centered AI track with a secondary core in Applied Data Science. This transition reflected a growing interest in not just building models, but building AI systems that people can actually use and trust. The Human-Centered AI track pushed me to think about how AI interacts with real users — questions of interface design, transparency, ethics, and the experience of someone who has never touched a dataset but needs to benefit from one.

This dual focus shaped all three of my portfolio projects. The Mental Health Analysis project required communicating sensitive findings to a non-technical stakeholder (the Barnes Center). The Music Recommendation project centered on a real user need — music discovery from short clips. The AI Tax Assistant was built explicitly for students without tax expertise, with privacy and usability at the center of every design decision. In each case, the technical work only mattered insofar as it served the people on the other end of it.

Overall synthesis

How I achieved all six outcomes

Across three projects, each program learning outcome was demonstrated — often multiple times, in different contexts.

PLO achievement summary

PLO 1
Data collection & storage: In the Music project, I built a full data pipeline from raw audio to normalized mel-spectrograms across 8,000 Free Music Archive tracks. In the Tax Assistant, I designed the chunking strategy that transformed raw IRS PDFs into a structured, retrievable vector database in ChromaDB — learning firsthand that upstream pipeline decisions directly impact downstream retrieval quality.
PLO 2
Actionable insight: The Mental Health project produced concrete recommendations for the Barnes Center — focus outreach on lonely students, build group programs, and use RAs for early identification. The Tax Assistant delivered real-time, personalized tax guidance to students who otherwise couldn't afford a CPA. Both projects moved from analysis to recommendations a real stakeholder could act on.
PLO 3
Visualization & prediction: The Mental Health project combined Random Forest classifiers with unsupervised clustering and t-SNE visualization to surface student risk profiles. The Music project used a deep learning autoencoder as a predictive embedding model. In both cases, visualization work was essential to making model outputs interpretable to the projects' intended audiences.
PLO 4
Programming: All three projects were built in Python. Across the portfolio I worked with scikit-learn, PyTorch, LangChain, ChromaDB, Streamlit, and HuggingFace — writing production-oriented code that needed to integrate cleanly with teammates' contributions, handle edge cases, and run reliably on real data.
PLO 5
Communication: Each project involved presenting findings to a non-expert audience. The Mental Health project was written for Barnes Center administration. The Tax Assistant's entire interface was designed for students with no tax background — using plain language, step-by-step checklists, and visual form guides. The Music project was presented as a research poster accessible to a general audience.
PLO 6
Ethics & fairness: The Mental Health project explicitly addressed the risk of misusing model outputs for individual diagnosis rather than group-level planning, and flagged limitations around campus generalizability. The Tax Assistant was built with privacy-by-design — ephemeral user data, PII masking, and clear legal disclaimers. Both projects treated ethics as a core design constraint, not an afterthought.
