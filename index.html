Program learning outcomes

What the program teaches

The MS in Applied Data Science is built around six core learning outcomes that go through the data science lifecycle.

PLO 1
Data collection & storage
Identify and leverage applicable technologies to collect, store, and access data at scale. In my own words: knowing how to find data, store it efficiently, and retrieve it when needed, whether that's a database, a vector store, or raw files.
PLO 2
Actionable insight
Create actionable insight across societal, business, and political contexts using the full data science life cycle. In my own words: the whole point of data science, turning numbers into decisions that actually help people.
PLO 3
Visualization & prediction
Apply visualization techniques and predictive models to help generate and communicate insight. In my own words: using charts, models, and visuals to make patterns visible and predictions useful.
PLO 4
Programming
Use programming languages such as R and Python to support the generation of actionable insight. In my own words: writing code that actually works and solves real problems, not just toy examples.
PLO 5
Communication
Communicate insights via visualization and analytics to a broad range of audiences including sponsors and technical leads. In my own words: being able to explain what you found to both a data scientist and someone who has never written code.
PLO 6
Ethics & fairness
Apply ethics in the development, use, and evaluation of data and predictive models, including fairness, bias, transparency, and privacy. In my own words: making sure the systems we build don't harm people, leak private data, or make unfair decisions.

Three projects that demonstrate my skillset

IST 707 — Applied Machine Learning
Mental Health & Wellbeing Analysis: Predicting Anxiety and Depression Among Syracuse Students
Analyzed responses from 4,957 Syracuse University students in the 2025 Campus Wellness Survey to predict anxiety and depression using machine learning. The goal was to help the Barnes Center shift from reactive crisis management to proactive, data-informed outreach, identifying at-risk students before they reach a crisis point. We built Random Forest classifiers achieving 72.55% accuracy for anxiety and 79.04% for depression, using validated clinical screening tools (GAD-2 for anxiety, PHQ-2 for depression) as target variables while excluding direct symptom measures to prevent data leakage and ensure the models could work proactively on students who hadn't yet disclosed problems.
My specific contribution

I led the clustering analysis portion of the project. I implemented and tuned both DBSCAN and HDBSCAN algorithms, ran a grid search across hyperparameters to optimize silhouette scores, and generated t-SNE visualizations to make the high-dimensional clusters interpretable. I then analyzed and wrote up the cluster profiles, identifying distinct student segments such as the all-male low-anxiety cluster, the lonely high-depression group falling through the cracks of campus services, and the therapy-engaged students with strong social support. These cluster insights formed the basis of our recommendations to the Barnes Center.

Stakeholder
Syracuse University Barnes Center for Health & Wellbeing
Key finding
Loneliness was the #1 predictor of both anxiety and depression — lonely students showed 2–5× higher rates than socially connected peers
Technologies
Python scikit-learn Random Forest DBSCAN HDBSCAN t-SNE

IST 691 — Deep Learning in Practice
Music Recommendations for Indie Artists Using Deep Learning
Built a Convolutional Autoencoder that analyzes short 5-second audio clips to identify acoustically similar songs, bridging mainstream and independent music catalogs. The core problem: listeners on TikTok and Instagram encounter 10–20 second clips but can't find the full track or discover similar indie artists. Existing tools like Shazam only match exact audio, and Spotify's recommendations favor popular artists. Our solution was a deep learning model that learns compact audio embeddings from short snippets, enabling similarity matching without relying on user behavior or popularity data.
My specific contribution

I was responsible for the audio data pipeline. I handled the preprocessing of 8,000 Free Music Archive tracks, splitting each song into 5-second chunks with 50% overlap to generate 83,290 training snippets, converting every chunk into a mel-spectrogram (a 128×216 array representing frequency and loudness over time using Short-Time Fourier Transform), and normalizing all spectrogram values between 0 and 1 so that loud and quiet songs would be comparable. I also implemented the song-level train/validation split to prevent data leakage, ensuring no chunks from the same song appeared in both sets.

Results
70% Top-10 accuracy; when the exact song wasn't retrieved, results were typically from the same genre, showing the embeddings captured meaningful musical similarity
Model
Convolutional Autoencoder with 256-dimensional bottleneck embeddings; validation loss of 0.0042 MSE
Technologies
Python PyTorch Convolutional Autoencoder Mel-spectrograms STFT Free Music Archive

IST 688 — Building Human-Centered AI Applications
AI Tax Assistant: RAG-Powered Chatbot for Federal Tax Guidance
Developed a full-stack AI chatbot that answers tax questions using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) over a custom IRS document vector database, with a specific focus on simplifying tax filing for students, especially international students. Built on Google Gemini 2.5 Flash and LangChain, the system uses a multi-agent architecture: an Orchestrator Agent directing the workflow, an Intake Agent building personalized tax profiles, a RAG Agent retrieving from ChromaDB, a Checklist Agent generating interactive task lists, and a Tool Agent running tax calculations. User data is treated as ephemeral, never logged to a database or used for training, and all uploaded documents are processed with PII detection and masking.
My specific contribution

I contributed approximately 25% of the total project effort across two areas. First, I implemented the chunking strategy for the RAG pipeline, dividing IRS documents into 900-token chunks with 250-token overlap, a configuration I arrived at through testing to preserve context across chunk boundaries without sacrificing retrieval precision. Second, I designed and built the Tool Agent, which provides a tax calculator computing federal tax liability based on income and filing status using 2024 tax brackets and standard deductions. I also initially developed a web search tool but removed it after team discussion to keep the system focused on our curated IRS documentation. Both components were merged into the main orchestrator codebase.

Actionable insight
Personalized, real-time tax guidance grounded in official IRS documents, accessible to students without tax expertise or access to a CPA
Ethics & privacy
Ephemeral user data; automatic PII detection and masking; legal disclaimer on every response; sessions user-controlled and deletable
Technologies
Python Streamlit LangChain Google Gemini RAG ChromaDB EasyOCR HuggingFace

Program track

My academic track

How I shaped my degree around the intersection of AI and data science.

Applied Human-Centered AI + Applied Data Science

From data science to human-centered AI

I began the program on the Applied Data Science track, which gave me a strong technical foundation, learning how to collect, process, and model data using Python, machine learning, and statistical methods. Courses like IST 707 showed me the fundamentals of building predictive models and interpreting their outputs for real stakeholders.

As the program progressed, I shifted to the Applied Human-Centered AI track with a secondary core in Applied Data Science. This transition reflected a growing interest in not just building models, but building AI systems that people can actually use and trust. The Human-Centered AI track pushed me to think about how AI interacts with real users, it made me ask questions about interface design, transparency, ethics, and the experience of someone who has never touched a dataset but needs to benefit from one.

This dual focus shaped all three of my portfolio projects. The Mental Health Analysis project required communicating sensitive findings to a non-technical stakeholder. The Music Recommendation project centered on a real user need, music discovery from short clips. The AI Tax Assistant was built explicitly for students without tax expertise, with privacy and usability at the center of every design decision. In each case, the technical work only mattered insofar as it served the people on the other end of it.

Reflections on the program

What I expected, what I learned, and what surprised me.

My journey through the MS in Applied Data Science

Background

I graduated from Indiana University's Kelley School of Business with a degree in Finance. Most of my undergrad was in investment analysis, financial modeling, and capital markets. I knew how to build financial models and read a balance sheet, but what kept standing out to me was how much decision-making depended on data that wasn't being used well. Meetings would take too long because the data was messy or not organized in a way that made the answer clear. The analysis existed, but it wasn't doing what it could.

Before starting the program, I had a research assistant role at Syracuse where I analyzed a smart building dataset in Python. Think of a smart building like a school that tracks how much electricity every room uses, every hour of every day. My job was to take all that raw data, clean it up, and find patterns in it. When I finally got it organized and turned into charts that showed real trends around energy use, I realized I didn't just want to read reports built on data. I wanted to build the systems that generate them. That's what brought me here. I wanted to close the gap between my finance background and the technical skills that would let me work with data at a level most finance people can't.

Going in, I expected the program to be mostly machine learning, training models, tuning settings, and getting deep into algorithms. That turned out to be partially true, but it missed a lot of what the program actually is.

What I expected vs. what I found

The biggest gap between expectation and reality was data preparation. Before you ever build a model, you're dealing with missing values, messy formats, and questions about how the data was collected in the first place. Think of it like trying to bake a cake when half your ingredients are unlabeled, some are expired, and a few are measured in the wrong units. You have to sort all of that out before you can actually start cooking. In every project I worked on in this program, that sorting-out phase took longer than the actual modeling. Every time, without exception.

I also didn't expect how tools-driven the learning would be. It's not just theory. You're always switching between R, SQL, Tableau, and Python depending on the problem. Each tool has its strengths, and learning how to pick the right one for the task, rather than defaulting to whatever you know best, was something I didn't fully anticipate. By the end of the program I had built pipelines in R, written queries in SQL, built dashboards in Tableau, and trained deep learning models in Python. That range felt practical in a way that purely theory-based courses wouldn't have.

The other thing I didn't anticipate was how much communication matters. The Mental Health project meant presenting results to a university health center that doesn't work with statistics day to day. Imagine explaining to your school principal not just that students are stressed, but exactly which students, why, and what the school could do about it. That's the level of clarity required. The Tax Assistant meant building something a first-time tax filer could actually use without a manual. The music project meant writing up deep learning results for a general audience. In almost every project, the final deliverable wasn't just the analysis. It was how clearly you could explain what the data means and why it matters.

Switching tracks

I started on the Applied Data Science track, which gave me what I needed technically. But as I moved through the program I kept running into the same question: a model can be accurate and still be useless if the person on the other end can't interpret it, trust it, or do anything with it. That question didn't fit neatly into the ADS track, and I found myself more interested in it than I expected.

I switched to Applied Human-Centered AI with ADS as a secondary core. That shift brought a different lens to everything. Instead of just asking "does the model work," it pushed me to ask "can a real person actually use this, and does it treat them fairly." It's the difference between building a calculator that gives correct answers and building one that a ten-year-old can figure out without a manual. Both matter. The second one is harder.

That switch also reflected something I was figuring out about my own direction. I originally thought I might go deeper into machine learning. But I realized I genuinely enjoy working closer to the business side, building dashboards, identifying trends, and helping teams make decisions. The Human-Centered AI track aligned better with where I was actually heading.

The program learning outcomes

PLO 1, data collection and storage, came through most directly in the music and tax projects. In the music project I built the data pipeline from scratch: 8,000 songs from a public music library, split into 5-second clips with overlapping windows, converted into a visual format called a mel-spectrogram that shows what a sound looks like as an image, then normalized so that loud and quiet songs could be compared fairly. In the tax project I designed the process that turned raw IRS documents into a searchable database the AI could pull from when answering questions. What both taught me is that the decisions you make early in a pipeline directly affect what's possible later. The settings I chose for how to slice up the IRS documents affected how well the system could find relevant answers weeks later.

PLO 2, actionable insight, was most visible in the mental health project. The Barnes Center didn't need a paper about model accuracy. They needed to know which students were falling through the cracks and why. Our analysis found a specific group of male students who were experiencing depression but had zero contact with campus mental health services. That's the kind of finding that could directly change how a university reaches out to students who need help. That's the difference between a finding and an insight. Not just what's true, but what someone can actually do with it.

PLO 3, visualization and prediction, showed me that a chart isn't just decoration. It's how you make a technical result mean something to someone who wasn't in the room when you built it. The cluster visualizations I made for the mental health project turned a table of statistics into something a health administrator could look at and immediately understand. Without the visual, the result stays stuck in a spreadsheet. With it, it becomes a conversation.

PLO 4, programming, ran across all three projects. scikit-learn and HDBSCAN in the machine learning project, PyTorch and spectrogram processing in deep learning, LangChain and ChromaDB and Streamlit in the AI application. Each required writing code that worked on real messy data, fit together with what teammates were building, and was clear enough that someone else could read it and understand what it does. That last part is harder than it sounds.

PLO 5, communication, was the skill I practiced most consistently across the program. Different audiences, different contexts, different standards for what counts as clear. The health project needed careful language that didn't overstate what the models could do. The tax assistant needed plain language at every step. The music poster needed visuals that were self-explanatory. By the end I had presented technical findings to a university health administration, written documentation for a student-facing AI application, and produced a research poster for a general audience, all in the same academic year.

PLO 6, ethics and fairness, produced the most important conversations. In the mental health project we spent real time on the risk of misusing the results. A model that labels a student as at-risk without a proper evaluation could do real harm, through stigma, wrong interventions, or misallocated resources. We built explicit limitations into our findings to reflect that. In the tax assistant, privacy was an architectural decision, not an afterthought. User data was never stored permanently, sensitive information like Social Security numbers was automatically detected and masked, and every response included a disclaimer that the system was educational guidance, not professional advice. Building those protections in from day one, rather than adding them later, is an approach I'll carry into every system I build going forward.

The three projects

The mental health project taught me that framing the problem is harder than solving it. Deciding which data to include and which to leave out required constant judgment. We were trying to identify at-risk students using information collected before they'd disclosed any problems, which is the right goal, but it limits how accurate the model can be in ways we had to be honest about. My specific contribution was the clustering analysis, where I grouped students by shared patterns and then figured out what those groups actually represented. What that taught me is that an algorithm can find a pattern but it can't tell you what the pattern means. That part is human work.

The music project taught me patience. The preprocessing alone, turning 8,000 songs into 83,290 labeled audio clips and converting each one into a visual representation the model could read, took significant time before we ever trained anything. But when the model worked well, achieving 70% accuracy in identifying the correct song from a 5-second clip, it was because that foundation was solid. You can't shortcut the early work.

The tax assistant taught me that complexity isn't the same as quality. I built one component of the system with more complicated architecture than it needed. After testing it and talking with teammates, we stripped it down to something simpler that worked just as well and was easier for everyone to work with. Knowing when to simplify rather than build on top of existing complexity is a skill that takes real experience to develop.

Internships

As a Health Analytics Graduate Intern at Syracuse University I built automated pipelines in R and SQL to process and standardize over 4,000 records, and developed dashboards in Power BI and Tableau that leadership used to track operational metrics. What the internship added was stakes. These weren't practice datasets. Getting them wrong had real consequences for the reports they fed into. It also showed me how much of analytics work is the infrastructure nobody talks about: pipelines, validation logic, documentation that makes the analysis repeatable months later. That work isn't exciting but it's what makes everything else reliable.

At the Center of Excellence I worked across data from over 40 sources, identifying inconsistencies and building detection processes for financial and operational reporting. Working at that scale taught me something you don't get in a classroom: real-world data is messy in ways that aren't obvious until you've spent real time with it. Two datasets that are supposed to describe the same thing can disagree with each other, and figuring out why is a skill that takes practice.

Favorite class

IST 664, Natural Language Processing. NLP is the field that teaches computers how to read, understand, and work with human language. The course built from the ground up, starting with how text gets broken into pieces a computer can process, moving through older statistical methods, and eventually reaching the transformer models that power tools like the one you might use to draft an email or search the web today. I came out of it understanding why those systems work the way they do, not just how to use them. That matters when something breaks, which it always does eventually.

NLP is also directly connected to where I want to work. Analyzing financial news, parsing earnings calls, generating automated reports, these are real applications in business intelligence and capital markets. The course gave me the foundation to do that work seriously, and it reinforced that analytics, not pure machine learning research, is the right direction for me.

Best parts and surprises

The best part of the program was working on real, messy datasets and turning them into something useful. None of the data came pre-cleaned. The process of starting with something raw and complicated, figuring out what's actually in it, and building something from it that a real person ends up using was the most rewarding part of the experience. It felt like actual work, not practice for work.

The people were a big part of it too. Working with classmates from different backgrounds pushed me to communicate more precisely and think about problems from angles I wouldn't have considered on my own. When you have to explain a technical result to someone with a completely different background, you get much better at understanding what actually matters about that result.

The biggest surprise was how much the program emphasized storytelling. I came in expecting technical rigor and got that. But I didn't expect that explaining findings clearly to non-technical audiences would be such a consistent requirement. Every major project ended with a deliverable aimed at someone who wasn't going to read a methods section. That's how the work actually functions outside a classroom, so it was the right call. It just wasn't what I expected going in.

The other surprise was how broad the problems were. I came in thinking mostly about finance applications and ended up working on problems in health, music, and tax law. That breadth was genuinely useful. It pushed me to think of data science as a general skill rather than something tied to one industry, which made me more flexible and more curious about what else the same tools could do.

Looking back

When I started, I could build a financial model in Excel and write basic Python. By the end, I had built machine learning pipelines, trained deep learning models on audio data, designed a multi-agent AI system, and built dashboards that real stakeholders used to make real decisions. That's a real distance to cover in two years.

More than any specific tool, what I'm taking out of the program is the ability to start with something messy and make it useful. The program also clarified my direction. I came in thinking I might go deep into machine learning research. I'm leaving pointed toward analytics engineering and business intelligence, working at the intersection of data and decision-making. That's where the work feels most meaningful to me, and the program gave me both the skills and the clarity to pursue it.

The Human-Centered AI track kept reminding me that the goal was never the model. It was always whoever was on the other end of it.
